My Learning Journey in AS Watson's Internship

From book-smart to workplace-ready.

This is my honest learning log from a Digital Innovation Internship at AS Watson. I came in with textbook confidence; I left with a better feel for judgement, teamwork, and the small habits that make work actually work.

Book-smart knowledge is a foundation. The workplace taught me what to do with it.
Explore the journey Read the takeaways Understand the pivots

01 About me

Who I am

I am Fong Wai Ming, a BEng Computer Science student at HKUST. During this internship, I worked around internal AI tools, support tasks, and plenty of team conversations. The technical side mattered, of course. But the bigger lesson was learning how people turn software from a clever idea into something others can trust.

02 Baseline

Where I started

Honestly, I used to think strong academic knowledge would carry most of the weight. HKUST gave me a useful foundation, and I still value that. But work has its own rhythm: unclear requests, busy people, security habits, pressure, and decisions that need more than a neat textbook answer.

What school gave me

I came in with coursework knowledge, technical curiosity, and the confidence to learn new tools when I had to. That helped. It gave me enough language to follow AI-related work and enough courage to ask better questions.

What work added

Then work added the messy parts: vague needs, end-user meetings, vendor comments, production responsibility, and the simple fact that other people depend on what you do.

Readiness is not just solving the task. It is knowing when to ask, when to slow down, when to align with others, and when to admit that a street-smart judgement call matters.

03 Learning Journey

First-time moments, month by month

The months below are not just a timeline of tasks. They are the moments where I kept thinking, "Oh, so this is how work really feels." Some were technical, yes, but the bigger learning came from meetings, ownership, vendor feedback, teaching, and the occasional uncomfortable lesson.

This was my first reminder that enterprise work has history. A system is not just code; it carries decisions, habits, and responsibilities from the people before me.

I started to see that AI ideas in business are valuable only when they connect to a real workflow, not when they are interesting only from a technical angle.

I learned that end-user conversations are not a formality. They are where technical assumptions meet practical needs.

The technology was new to me, but the larger lesson was that new tools still need careful judgement, testing, and communication before they are useful.

This was a practical step from idea to design. I began to understand how support tools can reduce repetitive work if they fit existing team habits.

SIT made the work feel less like a classroom demo. The question became not only whether it runs, but whether it is ready for a real environment.

Leadership was not a title. It meant being accountable for continuity, questions, support, and the confidence other people placed in the system.

I felt trusted, but this also created the early habit of moving alone too quickly. That habit later became one of my most important lessons.

Leading a meeting made me realize that preparation is not only knowing the content. It also means helping other people follow the direction clearly.

Collaboration changed the pace of work. It required patience, shared language, and the ability to align before implementation.

It reminded me that workplace learning is social too. Trust and comfort do not come only from tasks; they also come from shared ordinary moments.

Production changed the meaning of completion. It was no longer about finishing a task for myself; it was about releasing something other people may depend on.

I had to translate technical understanding into a conversation that made sense to others. This was a street-smart skill, not only a technical one.

The tool was exciting, but it also made me think about responsibility. AI-assisted coding still needs human judgement, verification, and ownership.

Vendor collaboration showed me that professional communication often happens across boundaries. Clear feedback matters when another team must act on it.

This was a practical reminder that workplace readiness includes security awareness. Knowing code is not enough if daily habits create risk.

Teaching forced me to organize what I knew. It also showed me that knowledge becomes more valuable when it can be passed on clearly.

Feedback needed to be specific, practical, and respectful. It was another reminder that communication quality affects technical outcomes.

I treat this as a serious reflection, not gossip. A colleague's departure made the workplace feel less abstract. Honestly, my first thought was, "Maybe it could be me someday." Then another thought followed: maybe some of my work could lead us, or others, there if I stop paying attention to people behind the process.

Handover taught me that good work should survive beyond one person. Documentation, context, and clarity matter when responsibility moves to someone else.

This lesson connected back to earlier mistakes. Working alone may feel fast, but shared review and communication prevent hidden risks from growing into larger delays.

04 Failures & Pivots

The lesson that changed my working style

Here's the thing: a real team does not work like an assignment brief. My biggest mistake was not a syntax error or a missing framework trick. It was a working habit. I tried to handle too much by myself, almost like I still had a clear brief and a quiet deadline.

Before

Solo speed felt efficient

I thought moving fast meant understanding the problem, building the thing, and showing progress. Simple, right? Not quite.

The problem

Hidden risk appeared late

In one chatbot-related task, I did not check enough with others early on. A few important steps were missed, the approach needed to change, and the delay was a very plain reminder that private progress can still be the wrong progress.

After

Communication became part of engineering

I now see review, discussion, and shared responsibility as part of technical quality. They are not extra steps around the work. They are the work.

05 Key Takeaways

What mattered most

These are the lessons I want to highlight most: not the list of tools, but the change in how I understand workplace readiness, or soft skills that we can't learn from bare text. Some lessons only become clear when real people are waiting for your work.

01

School knowledge is useful, but it is not the whole job.

Coursework helped me think in a technical way. Real work added timing, unclear needs, different stakeholders, and consequences that do not wait for a perfect answer.

02

Street-smart judgement matters.

Work rarely arrives as a perfectly written assignment. I had to ask, clarify, read the room a little, and make decisions when the information was still incomplete.

03

Communication is part of engineering quality.

Clear communication saves time. It catches wrong assumptions early and helps a team choose a better path before the wrong one becomes expensive.

04

Solo speed is not always real speed.

Working alone can feel fast at first. But if the direction is wrong, that early speed turns into rework. I learned that lesson the hard way.

05

Non-technical awareness is still crucial.

Security habits, vendor feedback, meetings, teaching, documentation, and production support all shaped how I performed. None of them felt like "extra" after a while.

06

AI still needs human judgement.

AI tools can help a team move faster, but they still need checking, context, and someone willing to take responsibility for the final result.

06 Technical Exposure

Tools I touched, kept in perspective

I did learn technical tools: Python, FastAPI, React, AI APIs, prompt tuning, Linux commands, and production-support workflows. Still, I do not want the tool list to be the main story. Tools helped me contribute. Judgement, communication, and responsibility helped me grow.

Python, FastAPI, API implementation, simple testing, and a clearer sense of backend work.

AI APIs, prompt tuning, agentic workflow ideas, and responsible AI-assisted coding.

React, Vite, Next.js, and the reminder that tools should feel usable to real people.

Linux commands, SSH.

07 Future Outlook

What I want to improve next

Next, I do not just want to build faster. I want to become more reliable: clearer in how I explain things, calmer when requirements are vague, and more careful when other people may depend on my work.

Practical engineering habits

Plan earlier, document better, review more often, and prepare handover with care.

AI-assisted work with responsibility

Use AI tools to support coding, while keeping human checking and judgement central.

Communication before implementation

Ask earlier, align sooner, and treat uncertainty as something to raise, not hide.
